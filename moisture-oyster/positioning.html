Brand Positioning

The strategic foundation for Moisture Oyster. Who we are, who we serve, and the space we own — before a single pixel of branding is designed.

Slayers Team · April 2026 · v1.0

01 — Who She Is

The Screen-Native Professional

Our primary customer, defined by Google Trends data, market analysis, and consumer behavior research.

She's 25–38, urban, and stares at screens 8–12 hours a day. She's discovering she has dry eyes but doesn't think of herself as someone with a medical condition — she thinks of herself as someone who works hard and takes care of herself. She buys Glossier and Aesop. She'd never put a clinical-looking bottle on her desk. She doesn't know Moisture exists yet. But when she sees it, she'll immediately know it's for her.

Demographics

Female skew, 25–38
Urban: LA, NYC, Miami, Chicago
Knowledge worker, screen-heavy career

Psychographics

Design-conscious, intentional about self-care
Curates her environment and routine
DTC-fluent (subscribes, shares, reviews)

Her World

Shops Glossier, Aesop, Drunk Elephant
Drinks AG1, uses a nice water bottle
Has opinions about fonts

Her Pain

Dry, tired eyes by mid-afternoon
Won't reach for something that looks clinical
Ignoring the problem because the solutions feel wrong

Search Behavior

"Best dry eye drops" — not "cheap dry eye drops"
"Preservative-free," "natural," "vegan," "cruelty free"
"Digital eye strain" (+24% growth)

Why Her First

Buys fast. Pays premium. Tells everyone.
Completely underserved by every existing brand.
Sharing is her default behavior.

Why This Audience Over All Others

She's the only audience that scores high on all five “who cares most” criteria simultaneously.

No other segment does. Here's the proof.

1. She Buys Fast

She's already a DTC buyer. She subscribes to skincare, orders supplements online, buys from Instagram ads. The purchase motion is muscle memory. A Gen Z student browses and forgets. A 45-year-old man goes to CVS out of habit. She converts.

2. She Pays Full Price

Google Trends confirmed it: “best dry eye drops” (interest: 70) crushes “cheap dry eye drops” (interest: 17). She already pays $28 for Drunk Elephant, $35 for AG1, $22 for Aesop hand wash. A $24 eye drop is not a price conversation — it's a value conversation. She pays premium without flinching.

3. She Tells Everyone

She posts routines on TikTok, photographs her desk for Instagram, sends links with “you NEED this.” Topicals and Dieux eye patches hit BREAKOUT growth almost entirely through this woman sharing with other women like her. A male developer with dry eyes uses the product silently. She is your free marketing engine.

4. She's Completely Underserved

Systane, Refresh, TheraTears, Visine — all talk to her like she's a patient. Their packaging would embarrass her on a Zoom call. The beauty world is entering the eye space (Topicals, Dieux, Cosrx — all BREAKOUT) but only with patches. Nobody has claimed the eye drop position in her world. You're first.

5. Your Entire Zag Lands Perfectly

The positioning — “designed for screen life, not the pharmacy aisle” — only works if the customer cares about design, has a curated routine, and finds pharmacy products unacceptable. A 55-year-old doesn't care what the bottle looks like. A Gen Z teen can't afford it. She is the only person for whom your differentiation is the primary reason to buy.

The Expansion Path

Starting with her doesn't mean staying with her. She's the wedge — the audience that builds the brand for you.

Launch

Screen-native professional women, 25–35. She buys, shares, and builds the brand for you.

Year 1

Broader screen workers (male, 25–40). They see it on her desk. The positioning pulls them in.

Year 2+

Contact lens wearers, Gen Z. Brand trust is built. They come because it already means something.

She's not the biggest market. She's the most leverage.

02 — What We Stand Against

The Enemy

Every iconic brand has a clear adversary. Not a competitor — a worldview. This is what Moisture exists to fight.

We stand against the idea that taking care of your eyes has to feel medical, ugly, or embarrassing.

The old model Eye care has been trapped in the pharmacy aisle for 40 years. White boxes, clinical language, forgettable brands. It treats dry eyes like a diagnosis, not a daily reality of modern life.
The assumption That people will put up with ugly, clinical products because "it's just eye drops." That no one cares what the bottle looks like. That function is enough.
The gap Skincare evolved from medicine to self-care. Supplements evolved from pharmacy to wellness. Oral care evolved from clinical to design-led. Eye care never did. We're here to close that gap.
We are not against Clinical science, ophthalmology, or medical eye care. We respect and build on the science. We're against the experience being stuck in the past while everything else in self-care moved forward.

03 — What We Believe

Brand Point of View

The Cultural Tension

Screens gave us everything — careers, connection, creativity. And they're quietly destroying our eyes. Nobody is talking about this in a way that feels real, cool, or urgent.

The Belief

Your eyes are part of your body. They deserve the same care and intention you give your skin, your gut, your mind. The clinical eye care industry has treated them like a medical problem. We treat them like something worth taking care of — beautifully.

The Zag

Every competitor says "relief." We say identity.

“Moisture is for people who live on screens
and refuse to let it show.”

08 — How We Sound

Brand Personality & Tone of Voice

Four traits that guide every word we write, every image we choose, every interaction we design. Each comes with a guardrail to keep us honest.

Confident

We know what we are and we don't apologize for it. We speak with quiet authority — the authority of a brand that's done its homework and made real choices.

Confident
not Cocky

Warm

We talk like a smart friend, not a brand. Approachable, empathetic, human. We acknowledge real feelings without being sentimental or patronizing.

Warm
not Cheesy

Knowing

We understand her world — the screen fatigue, the curated shelf, the 3pm slump. We reference her reality without over-explaining it. She reads our copy and thinks: “they get me.”

Knowing
not Clinical

Minimal

Minimal
not Cold

09 — What We Always Say

Key Messaging Pillars

Four repeatable themes that every piece of communication — ads, packaging, social, email, PR — should ladder up to. If a message doesn't connect to one of these, it doesn't go out.

Pillar 01

Screen-Era Self-Care

Your eyes are part of your body. In a world of 8-12 hour screen days, eye care isn't optional — it's the missing step in your routine.

Use in: Hero copy, awareness ads, PR angles, content marketing, social captions

Pillar 02

Clean Without Compromise

Preservative-free. Hyaluronic acid. Ingredients you can read and trust. The same standard you hold your skincare to — applied to your eyes.

Use in: Product pages, packaging, Amazon listings, ingredient stories, trust-building content

Pillar 03

Designed to Be Seen

This is not a bottle you hide in a drawer. It belongs on your desk, in your bag, on your shelf — because eye care should be as considered as everything else in your life.

Use in: Packaging design, unboxing content, lifestyle photography, brand partnerships, UGC

Pillar 04

Your Eyes, Your Routine

Not a fix. Not medicine. A daily habit — like your morning skincare or your evening wind-down. Drop in when you need it. Make it yours.

Use in: How-to content, routine-building campaigns, subscription messaging, email nurture
